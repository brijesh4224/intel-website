Choosing a Video Editing Desktop

  • The best desktop for video editing depends on the type of video content you’re creating and your final output needs.
  • Processor performance, graphics capabilities, screen resolution, RAM, and storage are all important video editing PC specs to consider.
  • Video editing desktops powered by the latest Intel® Core™ i7 and i9 processors give you the tools to create engaging, high-resolution video for everything from social media to the big screen.

Whether you’re editing a family video or working on a film, desktop computers deliver excellent performance per dollar and typically cost less than other form factors, such as laptops. They also provide seamless rendering with high-resolution displays, enough RAM to handle multitasking, and plenty of storage—all of which are key to unleashing your creativity.

Key Considerations

To start with, the best desktop for video editing depends on what type of content you’re creating. Making a how-to vlog series for YouTube requires far less processing power than editing a feature film or creating video for virtual reality. If you’re producing professional commercials, you’ll need a fast computer that can help you meet deadlines.

Your desktop PC should support all of your creative tools seamlessly. For example, video editing software requires a lot of RAM. You need even more when multitasking with creative applications, such as those used for creating graphics or manipulating still imagery.

You should also consider what types of external hard drives you’ll use. For small projects shot with a smartphone or for online-only purposes, you may not need an external drive. Where video files are relatively small, you can upload footage directly to your computer and edit from there.

Larger projects, however, require a lot more storage and processing horsepower. You’ll need to transfer working files to a fast storage drive that can act as a high-performance extension of your computer. For archiving finished projects and associated raw footage, a good bulk storage system can accommodate large file sizes.

Video Editing PC Specs

In general, the faster and more powerful the video editing PC specs, the better-quality output you’ll get. Let’s take a closer look at the basic specifications.

  • Processor. Your computer’s processor frequency, or clock speed, affects your ability to work with your content. The higher the frequency, the faster performance you’ll get for running creative applications and making edits to project files. The more complicated the editing project, the more processing power you need—especially if you’re editing extremely large files from 4K video footage. If you need to do color correction, work in layers, add special effects, import graphics, or add transitions, having a high-performance processor is key.
    Video project files need to be rendered between each editing pass. For example, you might cut your raw footage together, then render your project. Then you might add a graphics layer and render your project again. You might do a third pass to color correct, followed by another render. The processor’s core count affects your computer’s ability to render files and export final content. More cores deliver faster performance for these tasks.
  • Graphics card. Your computer’s graphics processing capabilities play a critical role in video editing. An integrated graphics card enables high-resolution display capabilities and an accurate color gamut for color correction. With ample performance for multiple monitors, you can have your active project timeline open on one screen and use the other for easy access to bins containing clips and other files.
  • Memory. When you’re under deadline pressure, your computer’s memory, or RAM , enables you to work fast, render huge files, and deliver high-quality results. Memory is different from storage, which is how you save files. Your computer may have plenty of storage, but if you don’t have enough memory, creative applications can crash. If you’re using multiple creative software programs at once, memory is even more important.
  • Display. Being able to see accurate colors is critical for editing video for any application, from posting social media clips to producing a professional documentary film. You need a computer screen with enough resolution to match your camera’s video footage. Standard high-definition (HD) video has a resolution of 1920x1080, and 4K video is four times the pixel resolution(3840x2160).
  • Storage performance and capacity. Video production generates a lot of very big files. Raw video footage gets even bigger once you add effects, graphics layers, and sound. You’ve got to have a high-performance storage solution for your working files, as well as something dependable for bulk storage.

    Storage drives fall into two categories. Hard disk drives (HDDs) have arms with several “heads” that read and write data to spinning disks. These drives are a cost-effective option for archiving final projects and raw footage. Solid-state drives (SSDs) have no moving parts. They dramatically reduce the time it takes to access your files since you don’t have to wait for platter rotation to start up. You can easily sift through raw footage and edit enormous files right from the drive.
  • I/O ports and connectivity. Video editing requires a computer with high-performance ports that allow your monitors, external storage, speakers, and other peripherals to power your creativity.

    Connection speed is also important. Computers with a 10 Gigabit Ethernet (10GbE) port let you transfer huge video files from your computer to your storage drives painlessly. Wi-Fi 6–enabled PCs allow you to upload video files to the cloud quickly and securely.
